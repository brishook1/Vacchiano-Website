William Vacchiano

Principal Trumpet, New York Philharmonic
Professor, The Juilliard School

1912 – 2005

Few trumpet players in the twentieth century shaped the landscape of American orchestral music as profoundly as William Vacchiano. As principal trumpet of the New York Philharmonic from 1942 to 1973, he performed under Arturo Toscanini, Bruno Walter, Dimitri Mitropoulos, Leonard Bernstein, and Pierre Boulez—and played in the world premieres of nearly 200 works. His contributions to the music world extend far beyond the stage: hundreds of orchestral recordings, numerous method books, and a teaching career spanning seven decades at The Juilliard School produced over 2,000 students who went on to shape orchestras, conservatories, and jazz stages around the world.

Quick Facts

31

Years as principal trumpet of the New York Philharmonic (1942–1973) — the longest continuous tenure in America at the time of his retirement

67

Years on the faculty of The Juilliard School (1935–2002), where he shaped generations of professional trumpeters

2,000+

Estimated private students taught over the course of his career, from 1935 to 2005

1934

The year he won auditions for the Metropolitan Opera and the New York Philharmonic on the same day — a feat never duplicated

  • Joined the New York Philharmonic in 1935 as third/assistant principal trumpet; appointed principal in 1942 by Bruno Walter
  • Performed under Toscanini, Barbirolli, Bruno Walter, Rodzinski, Stokowski, Mitropoulos, Bernstein, and Boulez
  • Served on faculty at The Juilliard School, Manhattan School of Music, Mannes College of Music, Queens College, and Columbia Teachers College
  • Honorary Doctorate from The Juilliard School (2003); International Trumpet Guild Highest Award of Merit (1984)
  • Co-inventor of the Alessi–Vacchiano straight mute

“Vacchiano was always an inspiration and an encouragement, even decades after his retirement. As I go on in my musical career, I want to be as willing as he was to hand the baton off and be able to do everything I can to lift that person up and say, ‘Go on, you can do it.’”

Philip Smith — Former Principal Trumpet, New York Philharmonic
