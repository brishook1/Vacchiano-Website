Life & Career

A Life in Music

William Vacchiano  ·  1912–2005

1903

Vacchiano's father, Rafaello Vacchiano, immigrated from Cicciano, Italy, to the United States through Ellis Island.

c. 1904

Rafaello brought his wife Anna and daughters Mary and Margarita to America after saving enough money for their passage.

1909

The Vacchiano family moved from Brooklyn, New York, to Portland, Maine.

May 23, 1912

William Anthony Vacchiano was born in Portland, Maine, the seventh of eight children of Rafaello and Anna Vacchiano.

c. 1918  (age 6)

Heard his first live opera performance — Verdi's La Traviata — at Portland City Hall Auditorium.

c. 1920–1921  (age 8–9)

After an accident involving a friend's baritone horn, he entered the music program of Mr. DiNobile and began intensive solfège study.

c. 1924  (age 12)

Began studying cornet with Frank Knapp.

c. 1926  (age 14)

Appointed by Knapp to third trumpet in the Portland Municipal Orchestra (today's Portland Symphony Orchestra).

c. 1928–1930

Traveled regularly to Boston for lessons with Boston Symphony principal trumpet Georges Mager. Also studied with Walter M. Smith, Louis Kloepfel, and Gustav Heim.

1930

Married Ethel Josephine LaParde, a piano major and clarinet minor at the Institute of Musical Art.

Early 1930s

Moved to New York intending to pursue accounting and attend St. John's University. Shortly after arriving, met a friend from Portland in Times Square who recommended he study with Max Schlossberg.

Early 1930s

Auditioned for Max Schlossberg and was offered a full scholarship to the Institute of Musical Art — predecessor of The Juilliard School.

1933

Traveled to the Chicago World's Fair, where Edward Llewellyn recognized him as a student of Max Schlossberg solely from his playing style.

1930s

Performed with Walter Damrosch's WPA orchestra during the Great Depression; toured with the George Barrere Symphony; served as principal trumpet at the Chautauqua Music Festival.

1935

One Day, Two Orchestras

In a feat never duplicated, Vacchiano won auditions for both the Metropolitan Opera Orchestra and the New York Philharmonic on the same day.

  • Joined the New York Philharmonic as third trumpet and assistant principal
  • Had also been engaged to play in the pit for Cole Porter's Anything Goes
  • Began teaching at The Juilliard School — a tenure that would last 67 years
  • Purchased a house in Flushing, New York where he would live for the rest of his life
1935–1937

Third trumpet and assistant principal, New York Philharmonic.

1935–2002

Faculty, The Juilliard School for 67 years.

1935–2005

Maintained an extensive private teaching studio in Flushing, New York.

1937

Son Ralph Vacchiano was born.

1937–1999

Faculty, Manhattan School of Music.

1937–1983

Faculty, Mannes College of Music.

1942

Appointed principal trumpet by Bruno Walter, beginning a 31-year tenure that would become the longest continuous principal trumpet appointment in Philharmonic history at the time of his retirement.

On November 14, 1943, a 25-year-old Leonard Bernstein made his emergency conducting debut with the Philharmonic after Bruno Walter fell ill. Vacchiano was principal trumpet that day.

1942–1973

Served as principal trumpet, performing under Toscanini, Barbirolli, Bruno Walter, Rodzinski, Stokowski, Mitropoulos, Bernstein, and Boulez. Played in the world premieres of nearly 200 works.

1944

Daughter Jo Ann Vacchiano was born.

1952

Completed a bachelor's degree in trumpet performance at Manhattan School of Music.

1953

Completed a master's degree in trumpet performance at Manhattan School of Music.

1970–1973, 1991–1994

Faculty, Queens College.

1973

Retired from the New York Philharmonic after 38 years, ending a 31-year tenure as principal trumpet. Transitioned into a teaching and publishing focused phase of life.

1973–1976

Faculty, North Carolina School of the Arts.

1978

Received the New York Brass Conference for Scholarships Journal Award of Recognition.

1984

Received the International Trumpet Guild Honorary Award.

1991

The Juilliard School established the William Vacchiano Award in Trumpet.

1990s–early 2000s

Published nineteen trumpet method and etude books. Continued teaching well into his nineties.

May 23, 2003

Received an Honorary Doctorate from The Juilliard School on his 91st birthday.

September 19, 2005

William Vacchiano died at Cabrini Medical Center in Manhattan. He was 93.

2006

Brian Shook completed the doctoral dissertation William Vacchiano: His Career as a Trumpet Player and Pedagogue, which became the basis for Vacchiano's biography.

April 2011

Last Stop, Carnegie Hall: New York Philharmonic Trumpeter William Vacchiano by Brian A. Shook, with a foreword by Wynton Marsalis, published by the University of North Texas Press.
